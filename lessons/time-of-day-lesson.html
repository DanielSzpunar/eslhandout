ESL Lesson: Prepositions For Time Of Day

  • Home
  • Lessons
  • Time of Day Prepositions

Difficulty Level: ★ ★ ★ ☆ ☆


It is very important to use the correct preposition when discussing different times of the day. However, sometimes ESL students can become easily confused between "at" and "in the" when referring to time. In this lesson we will solve this problem once and for all!


The chart below is a useful reference guide for choosing the correct preposition for the time of day.

at in the
2 o'clock morning
breakfast time afternoon
lunchtime daytime
dinnertime middle of the day
teatime / snack time evening
night middle of the night
break time

Preposition: AT

Always use the preposition "at" when you are talking about a specific time. Also, use "at" when we are talking about a specific time of day (any point in time that includes the word "time") with the exception of daytime.


Correct Examples:

The movie will be over at 3:00 pm.

At lunchtime, I usually eat a small snack and go for a walk.

Our whole family eats together at dinnertime.

My co-workers and I only have enough time for a coffee and a cigarette at breaktime.


Incorrect Examples:

I usually read in the night.

It's not good to eat junk food in the breakfast time.


Preposition: IN THE

We use the preposition "in the" most commonly when we generalize about the morning, afternoon, and evening:

"I normally eat breakfast in the morning with my parents".

"This" can be used in exchange of "in the" if we are talking about a specific time:

"I normally eat breakfast in the morning with my parents, but this morning I ate breakfast alone."

Strangely, we use the preposition "at" for night, but "in the" for middle of the night, so be careful.


Correct Examples:

It's best for me to study English twice a day, in the morning, and again in the evening, after dinner.


Incorrect Examples:

My brother usually walks the dog at the morning, while I walk the dog at the afternoon.


Tips:

1.) this morning > this afternoon > this evening > TONIGHT (not this night)

yesterday morning > yesterday afternoon > yesterday evening > LAST NIGHT (not yesterday night)

BUT...

tomorrow morning > tomorrow afternoon > tomorrow evening > tomorrow night

2.) Never say "next, next day" or "next, next week". Instead, say "in two days" or "two days from now."

"He'll be returning from China in two weeks."



Quiz:

Fill in the blank with the correct answer using either "at", "in the", or "this".

  • 1.) I'll be going to China at the end of ____ week.
  • 2.) We try to eat as a family _____ dinnertime.
  • 3.) Due to my companies international presence, it's not uncommon to recieve emails ______ middle of the night.
  • 4.) I haven't received an email ____ night in a long time.
  • 5.) It takes about twenty minutes to get ready _____ morning, but only 10 minutes to get ready for bed _____ night.
  • 6.) Our staff eats together _____ lunchtime but ______ evening we go our separate ways _____ dinnertime.
  • 7.) It's not easy taking pictures _____ night.
  • 8.) _____ evening I didn't eat ____ dinnertime.
  • 9.) I usually have to use the washroom _____ middle of the night.
  • 10.) My curfew is _____ 3 ______ morning.



Questions:

Answer the questions in full sentences. Even though they are easy be mindful of the proper preposition you have to use.

1.) When do you have dinnertime?

2.) What time do you usually wake up and go to bed?

3.) During days off, and weekends, how do you fill your time in the middle of the day?

4.) What time does your work shift start and finish?

5.) How do you unwind after work in the evening?

6.) When was your curfew growing up?


Daily Expressions & Idioms:

1.) "In the meantime": Means until something expected happens.

"You're new computer will arrive in the evening. In the meantime, you can use my laptop". "The election results won't be televised until after 7pm. In the meantime, let's watch the baseball game."

2.) "Above and beyond": More than is required.

"He's a great worker. He often goes above and beyond what is expected of him."
